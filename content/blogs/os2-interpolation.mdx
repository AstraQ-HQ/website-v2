The maritime domain is defined by silence. While the Automatic Identification System (AIS) acts as the digital heartbeat of global shipping, the pulse is irregular. A vessel transmits its coordinates at `14:32:07`, and then, due to signal interference, satellite blind spots, or deliberate obfuscation, it may vanish until `14:47:33`.

In that fifteen-minute void, the vessel does not cease to exist. It moves. It accelerates. It turns against the resistance of the waves.

The final challenge of Maritime Domain Awareness is not **observation**, but **reconstruction**. It is the mathematical imperative to fill the silence between the signals. This volume explores the **Interpolation Engine** of OS²: a system that rejects the opacity of deep learning in favor of the transparency of cubic splines and kinematic physics.

## The Fallacy of Linear Assumption

The standard industry approach to missing data is **Linear Interpolation**. When a system possesses two points, it draws a straight line between them. This assumes that a ship travels on rails, immune to the forces of nature.

This assumption insults physical reality. Ships possess mass and inertia. They contend with drag coefficients and turning radii. A supertanker cannot instantaneously alter its heading by $40 \degree$ without the gradual arc demanded by hydrodynamics.

To rely on linear interpolation is to accept a fractured reality of sharp zig-zags and instantaneous velocity shifts. To achieve true awareness, the system must model the **physics of the hull** moving through water.

## The Architecture of Continuity: Cubic Spline Synthesis

OS² treats trajectory reconstruction not as a pattern-matching problem, but as a constraint optimization problem. We employ **Cubic Spline Interpolation**, constructing piecewise third-degree polynomials between consecutive data points.

Why splines? Because a vessel in motion adheres to three fundamental continuities, which the spline algorithm enforces mathematically:

1. **Position Continuity:** The path remains unbroken. The vessel does not teleport.
2. **Velocity Continuity:** There are no sudden jumps in speed. The momentum is conserved.
3. **Acceleration Continuity:** There are no infinite forces. The turn rate is smooth.

The algorithm solves a tridiagonal matrix system to define a curve that minimizes "bending energy". It finds the path of least resistance: the path a physical object would naturally take through a fluid medium.

## The Inertia of Speed: Quadratic Modeling

Predicting **Speed Over Ground (SOG)** requires a different physical model. Velocity changes more gradually than position. A vessel applying thrust experiences a delay before reaching cruising speed; conversely, a vessel cutting engines glides before stopping.

We model this behavior using **Quadratic Interpolation**. By fitting a parabola to neighboring speed reports, the system captures the **"Overshoot Effect"**.

Consider a ship accelerating from 0.4 knots to 9.2 knots. A linear model assumes a constant ramp-up. The quadratic model detects the inertia, correctly predicting that the vessel likely peaked slightly above the final stabilized speed before settling. This nuance distinguishes a mechanical simulation from a mere data plot.

## The Geometry of the Turn: Angular-Aware Interpolation

**Course Over Ground (COG)** presents the most complex challenge due to the circular nature of compass headings. A turn from $350^\circ$ to $10^\circ$ is a simple $20^\circ$ adjustment to the starboard, not a massive $-340^\circ$ rotation to the port. Standard algorithms often fail this **"Wraparound"** test.

Furthermore, a rudder does not bite into the water instantly. The rate of turn (ROT) starts slowly, accelerates through the maneuver, and dampens as the new course is established.

To model this, OS² replaces linear angular mixing with a **Smooth-Step Easing Function**:

$$s(u) = 3u^2 - 2u^3$$

This equation creates a sigmoid curve that precisely mimics the hydrodynamic response of a rudder-steered vessel. It eliminates the robotic angular snaps of standard tracking tools, replacing them with the organic curves of actual navigation.

## The Rejection of Flat Earth Geometry

In the open ocean, the curvature of the Earth renders Euclidean geometry obsolete. Calculating distances on a flat plane introduces unacceptable errors at high latitudes.

OS² enforces a strict **Haversine-only** protocol. All internal metrics, distance, velocity vectors, and spatial separation, are computed using spherical trigonometry.

$$d = 2R \cdot \arcsin\left(\sqrt{\sin^2\left(\frac{\Delta \phi}{2}\right) + \cos \phi_1 \cos \phi_2 \sin^2\left(\frac{\Delta \lambda}{2}\right)}\right)$$

This ensures that the predicted trajectory holds true whether the vessel navigates the equator or the Arctic circle.

## Performance and Robustness

The efficacy of this physics-based approach is validated through rigorous stress testing. In trials using AIS data with **95% random packet loss** (simulating a hostile or highly degraded environment), the OS² interpolation engine achieved an RMSE (Root Mean Square Error) of **0.0078**, significantly outperforming standard Linear, Hermite, and Kalman Filter approaches.
